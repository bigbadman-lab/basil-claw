Executive Summary

The paper argues that Britain’s pub sector is in sustained decline, with approximately 15,800–16,000 net closures since 2000.

It identifies high taxation, business rates, employment costs, VAT, and energy prices as primary structural pressures.

It proposes reforms to business rates, VAT, alcohol duties, and National Insurance contributions to stabilise the sector.

It recommends expanding investment incentives and apprenticeship reforms to strengthen long-term sustainability.

It advocates targeted licensing changes and clear meat labelling to support community cohesion and British agriculture.

The paper presents its proposals as immediately actionable measures within existing government frameworks.

It frames pubs as economically significant (contributing £93 billion annually and employing 3.5 million people) and socially important institutions.

Problem Definition

The paper identifies a sustained structural crisis in Britain’s pub and hospitality sector:

Net pub closures since 2000: estimated 15,800–16,000 (approximately 25–35% decline).

Pub numbers reduced from approximately 60,800 in 2000 to 44,000–45,000 by mid-2025.

2024: 412 closures in England and Wales.

2023: 509 closures nationwide.

Over 2,283 closures in England and Wales since 2020.

1/3 of hospitality outlets operating at a loss.

76% increased prices.

63% reduced staff hours.

1/3 restricting opening hours.

4/10 reducing investment.

Primary structural pressures identified:

High business rates based on rateable values and rising multipliers.

20% VAT on hospitality sales, higher than many European countries.

Alcohol duties among the highest globally, uprated annually.

Increased employer National Insurance contributions and reduced thresholds.

Rising minimum wage and employment costs.

Energy price increases.

Lockdown-related debt burdens.

Food and drink inflation exceeding 20% in recent years.

Key Proposals / Recommendations
Proposal: Business Rates Reform

Objective: Reduce rate burdens and prevent further pub closures.

Mechanism:

Reinstate 40% Retail, Hospitality and Leisure (RHL) relief.

Freeze rateable value increases.

Set business rates multiplier at 30p.

Introduce a Rural Heritage Exemption.

Expected Impact:

Prevent projected 63% bill increases.

Reduce disproportionate burden compared to warehouses and supermarkets.

Implementation Notes:

Operates within existing NNDR framework.

Would reverse scheduled 2026 reforms affecting RHL properties.

Proposal: Tiered VAT Reduction (12.5%)

Objective: Stimulate demand and improve cash flow in hospitality.

Mechanism:

Reduce VAT from 20% to 12.5% for hospitality sales.

Potential community event incentives tied to rebates.

Expected Impact (based on cited analysis):

182,400 jobs over five years.

£4.9 billion additional turnover over five years.

Positive Treasury returns within five years.

Implementation Notes:

Temporary reduced rates previously implemented during COVID-19 response.

Would apply sector-wide.

Proposal: Alcohol Duty Reform (Draught Relief Expansion)

Objective: Lower cost of draught products and support domestic producers.

Mechanism:

Increase Draught Relief from 9.2% to at least 20%.

Consider higher expansion (up to 50%).

Expand Small Producer Relief.

Expected Impact:

20% relief: 2,251 additional FTE jobs; £70m economic boost.

50% relief: 8,584 FTE jobs; £265m boost; 70m additional pints sold.

Implementation Notes:

Builds on 2023 alcohol duty reform framework.

Applies to products over 1.2% ABV.

Proposal: Extend Enterprise Investment Scheme (EIS)

Objective: Attract private investment into closed or distressed pubs.

Mechanism:

Extend EIS eligibility to operating hospitality businesses.

Apply criteria to pubs closed within three years or operating at a loss for 2+ years.

Maintain existing EIS requirements.

Expected Impact:

Incentivise reopening, refurbishment, and innovation.

Mobilise local and angel investors.

Implementation Notes:

Administered through HMRC.

Three-year post-investment trading requirement.

Anti-abuse safeguards required.

Proposal: Nationwide “Buy British” Small Producer Scheme

Objective: Support British alcohol producers supplying hospitality.

Mechanism:

Expand Small Producer Relief (SPR) threshold (under 4,500 hl).

Tapered discount up to 50% for smallest producers.

Extend to higher-ABV products not currently covered.

Expected Impact:

Lower supply costs.

Support rural producers and British agriculture.

Implementation Notes:

Aligns with current SPR model.

Applies to British-made packaged alcohol sold to hospitality.

Proposal: National Insurance Threshold Lift

Objective: Reduce employment cost pressures in hospitality.

Mechanism:

Raise employer NIC threshold from £5,000 to £15,000.

Annualise NIC calculations for seasonal workers.

Introduce one-year NIC holiday for hiring unemployed individuals.

Expected Impact:

Reverse sector-wide cost increases estimated at £3.4bn in 2025.

Mitigate projected job losses (84,000+).

Implementation Notes:

Requires HMRC payroll adjustments.

Sector-specific threshold adjustment proposed.

Proposal: Apprenticeship Reform

Objective: Improve completion, retention, and training outcomes in hospitality.

Mechanism:

Completion bonus: £1,500 to apprentice; £1,000 to employer.

Retention bonus: £2,500 shared after two years.

Reduce minimum duration to 8 months.

Remove age limit of 25.

Include hospitality in Growth and Skills Levy priority sectors.

Expected Impact:

Improve completion rates (currently 50–60%).

Increase retention beyond 65%.

Implementation Notes:

Delivered through DfE apprenticeship service.

Pilot programmes proposed from 2026.

Proposal: Licensing Act Update

Objective: Expand supervised social participation for 16–17-year-olds.

Mechanism:

Allow licensed venues to serve <4% ABV draught products to 16-year-olds without adult accompaniment.

Impose drink limits.

Expected Impact:

Expand customer base.

Provide supervised social settings.

Implementation Notes:

Requires licence variation.

Local authority oversight.

Designated Premises Supervisor training updates.

Proposal: Meat Labelling Requirements

Objective: Support informed consumer choice and British farming standards.

Mechanism:

Mandate clear labelling of slaughter methods (stun vs non-stun).

Expected Impact:

Increase transparency.

Support British farming standards.

Implementation Notes:

Applies to hospitality meat sourcing disclosures.

Legal / Structural Considerations

Business rates governed under National Non-Domestic Rates framework.

Alcohol duty structured under 2023 ABV-based reform.

Licensing Act 2003 requires statutory amendment or licence variation changes.

EIS and SEIS operate under HMRC-administered tax relief frameworks.

Apprenticeship Levy transitioning to Growth and Skills Levy in April 2026.

Proposals require coordination between HM Treasury, HMRC, DfE, and local authorities.

Operational / Logistical Considerations

Business rate relief changes tied to 2026 revaluation cycle.

VAT changes administratively straightforward (precedent during COVID).

EIS extension requires pub-specific eligibility verification.

Apprenticeship bonuses require payroll tracking and HMRC verification.

Licensing update requires local authority training and compliance oversight.

Small Producer Relief expansion requires duty recalibration.

Risks, Criticisms, or Trade-offs

Fiscal cost to Treasury from VAT and duty reductions (though projected long-term gains).

Risk of abuse under extended EIS eligibility without safeguards.

Licensing reform may attract public health or safety concerns.

Apprenticeship bonuses require monitoring to prevent misuse.

Business rate reforms may alter revenue distribution across sectors.
