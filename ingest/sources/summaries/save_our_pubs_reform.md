Executive Summary

Proposes a fiscally neutral five-point plan to support British pubs and the hospitality sector.

Attributes pub closures to high taxation, increased business rates, employer National Insurance increases, and energy costs.

States that pub numbers have declined from around 69,000 to around 45,000 by the end of 2024.

Commits to reducing VAT for hospitality to 10% and cutting beer duty by 10%.

Proposes scrapping the employer National Insurance increase for hospitality businesses.

Plans to abolish business rates for pubs on a staggered basis over four years.

Proposes regulatory reform of the “beer orders” to address tied pub contracts.

States that the measures will be funded by reinstating the two-child limit on Universal Credit, with a targeted exemption.

Problem Definition

The paper states that British pubs have experienced long-term decline, with numbers falling from approximately 69,000 to 45,000 by the end of 2024. It cites projections suggesting numbers could fall to 10,000 by 2040.

It identifies contributing factors including:

High levels of VAT and alcohol duty relative to other European countries.

Increases in employer National Insurance contributions.

Significant business rate increases, including a reported 76% rise affecting pubs.

Energy cost increases linked to net zero policies.

Structural issues in the tied pub model, where pubcos impose restrictive supply contracts.

The paper states that around 32% (£1.52) of a £4.80 pint consists of tax. It argues that continued closures affect employment, community life, and local economies.

Key Proposals / Recommendations
Proposal: Reduce VAT to 10% for Hospitality

Objective: Lower tax burden on pubs and create greater parity with supermarkets.

Mechanism: Reduce VAT across the hospitality sector from 20% to 10%.

Expected Impact: Lower consumer prices, improved competitiveness, and increased demand.

Implementation Notes: Costing assumes full pass-through to consumers and a price elasticity of -0.6.

Proposal: Scrap Employer National Insurance Increase

Objective: Reduce labour costs for hospitality businesses.

Mechanism: Reverse the increase in Employers’ National Insurance from 15% to 13.8%.

Expected Impact: Lower wage costs and support sector employment.

Implementation Notes: UK Hospitality estimates cited an additional £1 billion in wage costs from the increase.

Proposal: Cut Beer Duty by 10%

Objective: Reduce the tax component of beer prices.

Mechanism: Implement a 10% reduction in beer duty rates.

Expected Impact: Lower cost per pint and increased sales volumes.

Implementation Notes: Adjusted for secondary VAT effects since VAT is levied on duty-inclusive prices.

Proposal: Staggered Abolition of Business Rates for Pubs

Objective: Eliminate business rates burden on pubs.

Mechanism: Gradual abolition over four years, prioritising high street venues; full coverage by 2029–30.

Expected Impact: Reduced fixed costs and improved viability of pub businesses.

Implementation Notes: Costed at £10,733 per pub per annum; coverage expands annually as funding permits.

Proposal: Reform of Beer Orders and Tied Model

Objective: Support local ownership and improve publican earnings.

Mechanism: Liberalise legislation governing tied contracts between pubcos and landlords.

Expected Impact: Greater purchasing freedom and improved margins for tied publicans.

Implementation Notes: Notes that tied publicans paid 60–80% more for certain beer kegs compared to open market purchases; over 30% of pubs have tied contracts.

Legal / Structural Considerations (if applicable)

Reform of the Supply of Beer (Tied Estate) Order 1989 and related “beer orders”.

Repeal of net zero laws proposed to reduce energy costs.

Changes to tax legislation covering VAT, employer National Insurance, beer duty, and business rates.

Reinstatement of the two-child limit on Universal Credit, with a targeted exemption for households where both parents are UK nationals in full-time employment.

Operational / Logistical Considerations (if applicable)

Fiscal modelling presented for 2026–27 to 2029–30.

VAT reduction costed based on assumed demand elasticity and full consumer pass-through.

Employer NI changes partly offset by projected employment growth.

Business rate abolition phased in line with projected welfare savings.

Funding derived from estimated welfare savings of approximately £3 billion by 2029/30.

Risks, Criticisms, or Trade-offs (if mentioned)

Gross hospitality support costs range from £2.29 billion to £2.98 billion annually over the projection period.

Fiscal neutrality depends on projected savings from reinstating the two-child limit.

Assumptions include behavioural responses such as increased demand and employment.
