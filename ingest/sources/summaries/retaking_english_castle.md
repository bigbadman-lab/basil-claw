Executive Summary

The paper argues that Britain has moved from “ordered freedom” to a condition described as “anarcho-tyranny,” in which serious offenders are treated leniently while law-abiding citizens face increasing legal constraints.

It contends that traditional self-defence rights and the principle that “an Englishman’s home is his castle” have been weakened through legislation and judicial interpretation.

The document proposes five legislative reforms aimed at strengthening self-defence rights and restoring legal protections for householders.

These reforms include legalising pepper spray, amending the Occupiers’ Liability Act (1984), repealing Scotland’s Hate Crime and Public Order Act (2021), preserving jury trials, and requiring Attorney General consent for certain self-defence prosecutions.

The proposals are presented as restorations of earlier legal principles rather than new institutional innovations.

The paper asserts that the recommended measures would require no new bureaucracy and could be implemented within a single Parliament.

Problem Definition

The paper identifies several interrelated issues:

Erosion of self-defence rights: Legislative restrictions (e.g., Firearms Act 1968; Offensive Weapons Act 2019) prohibit civilians from carrying pepper spray and similar defensive tools.

Civil liability exposure for homeowners: The Occupiers’ Liability Act (1984) imposes duties of care toward trespassers, including criminal intruders, enabling civil claims in cases where burglars are injured.

Limitations on household privacy in Scotland: The Hate Crime and Public Order (Scotland) Act 2021 permits prosecution for certain speech conducted within private dwellings and authorises warrants for home searches.

Threat to jury trials: Proposals to replace jury trials with judge-only trials in most criminal cases are described as undermining long-standing legal traditions and potentially disadvantaging defendants in self-defence cases.

Ambiguity in self-defence law: Judicial interpretation of the Criminal Justice and Immigration Act (2008) and Crime and Courts Act (2013), particularly following the Collins case (2016), is described as creating uncertainty about what constitutes “reasonable,” “disproportionate,” or “grossly disproportionate” force.

Prosecutorial discretion: The Crown Prosecution Service (CPS) is said to have broad discretion in bringing charges against individuals claiming self-defence or acting under citizen’s arrest provisions.

Key Proposals / Recommendations
Proposal: Legalise Pepper Spray

Objective: Provide law-abiding adults, particularly vulnerable individuals, with access to a non-lethal means of self-defence.

Mechanism: Repeal s. 5(b) of the Firearms Act (1968) and s. 6 of the Offensive Weapons Act (2019), which classify pepper spray as a prohibited weapon or corrosive substance.

Expected Impact: Enable civilians to carry pepper spray legally for defensive purposes, reducing reliance solely on physical force.

Implementation Notes: No new regulatory body proposed; reform framed as a statutory repeal.

Proposal: Enact the Stanley Amendment to the Occupiers’ Liability Act (1984)

Objective: Remove civil liability for homeowners toward individuals committing or intending to commit criminal offences on their premises.

Mechanism: Insert the previously rejected amendment proposed by Lord Stanley of Alderley:

“No duty is owed by virtue of this section by an occupier of premises to any person who is on those premises having committed or with the intention of committing there a criminal offence of which that person is capable of being convicted.”

Expected Impact: Prevent civil damages claims by burglars or trespassers injured during the commission of crimes.

Implementation Notes: Amendment would clarify s. 1 of the 1984 Act without full repeal; other provisions of the Act would remain intact.

Proposal: Repeal Scotland’s Hate Crime and Public Order Act (2021)

Objective: Restore privacy within the home and remove criminal liability for certain speech conducted in private dwellings.

Mechanism: Repeal the Hate Crime and Public Order (Scotland) Act 2021 in full; use powers under s. 35 of the Scotland Act (1998), with amendment if necessary to clarify repeal authority.

Expected Impact: Eliminate the offence of “stirring up hatred” as defined in the 2021 Act and remove associated search and prosecution powers.

Implementation Notes: Repeal framed as consistent with parliamentary sovereignty.

Proposal: Preserve Jury Trials in Criminal Cases

Objective: Maintain jury trials, particularly in cases involving self-defence or defence of property.

Mechanism: Reinstitute jury trials if abolished by legislative reform; oppose replacing juries with single judges in most criminal cases.

Expected Impact: Ensure defendants in self-defence cases are judged by juries rather than exclusively by professional judges.

Implementation Notes: No specific draft legislation included; commitment to reverse planned reforms.

Proposal: Require Attorney General Consent for Self-Defence Prosecutions

Objective: Prevent what the paper characterises as overzealous or ideological prosecutions in self-defence and citizen’s arrest cases.

Mechanism:

Amend s. 43 of the Crime and Courts Act (2013) to require Attorney General consent before instituting proceedings alleging excessive force in self-defence (householder and non-householder cases).

Amend s. 3 of the Criminal Law Act (1967) to require Attorney General consent before prosecuting individuals alleged to have exceeded reasonable force in preventing crime or effecting arrest.

Expected Impact: Introduce an additional procedural safeguard before charges can be brought in self-defence-related cases.

Implementation Notes: Modelled on existing consent provisions (e.g., Public Order Act 1986 s. 27); framed as a check on CPS discretion.
